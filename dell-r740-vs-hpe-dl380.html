Refurbished Dell PowerEdge R740 vs HPE ProLiant DL380 Gen10: Which 2U Server Wins?

Short answer: a refurbished Dell PowerEdge R740 wins on GPU and NVMe flexibility, while the HPE ProLiant DL380 Gen10 wins on iLO management polish and broad drive-bay options. Both are excellent dual-socket 2U workhorses — the right pick depends on your workload, not the badge.

If you are buying refurbished servers India businesses trust for production, these two platforms cover the vast majority of dual-socket needs. Let us compare them honestly, spec by spec.

What CPUs and RAM do they support?

Both servers run dual Intel Xeon Scalable processors — 1st Gen (Skylake) and 2nd Gen (Cascade Lake). That means anything from a modest Xeon Silver 4210 up to a Xeon Gold 6248R or Platinum 8280, depending on your budget and thermal needs.

For memory, both platforms expose 24 DIMM slots and officially top out around 3TB of RDIMM/LRDIMM (more with Intel Optane PMem on supported configs). In practice, most buyers land on 128GB to 512GB DDR4 ECC, which is plenty for virtualization, databases, and app hosting.

The CPU and RAM story is close to a tie. The differences show up in storage, GPU, and management — so that is where you should focus.

Drive bays, NVMe, and storage flexibility

The R740 and DL380 Gen10 both offer 2.5-inch and 3.5-inch chassis variants. The R740 commonly ships as 8x or 16x 2.5-inch SFF, and the larger R740xd pushes to 24 SFF or 12 LFF for storage-heavy roles. The DL380 Gen10 mirrors this with 8/16/24 SFF and 12 LFF box options.

Where the R740 pulls slightly ahead is NVMe density — it supports up to 24 direct-attached NVMe drives on the right backplane, which matters for high-IOPS databases and caching tiers. If your roadmap includes a SAN storage backend or heavy all-flash arrays, the R740's NVMe headroom is a real advantage.

Both use hardware RAID — Dell PERC H730P/H740P, HPE Smart Array P408i. Both are mature, battery-backed, and well-supported.

GPU support: which handles accelerators better?

This is the R740's standout. It supports up to 3 double-width GPUs (or 6 single-width), making it a popular base for inference, rendering, and light training. If you are building GPU servers or entry-level AI servers, the R740 gives you more accelerator room in 2U.

The DL380 Gen10 supports GPUs too — typically up to 2-3 double-width cards depending on riser and power configuration — but the R740 is the more common choice when GPU density is the priority. For pure CPU workloads, this difference does not matter at all.

iDRAC vs iLO: which management is better?

Both ship enterprise-grade out-of-band management: Dell iDRAC9 on the R740, HPE iLO 5 on the DL380 Gen10. Both give you full remote KVM, virtual media, power control, health monitoring, and Redfish API access — usually requiring an Enterprise/Advanced licence for the virtual console.

iLO 5 has a reputation for a cleaner web UI and slightly smoother first-boot experience. iDRAC9 is deeply scriptable and integrates tightly with Dell's lifecycle tooling. Honestly, both are excellent; if your team already knows one ecosystem, stay with it.
